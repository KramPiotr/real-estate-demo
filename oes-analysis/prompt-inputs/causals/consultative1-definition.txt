# Behaviour Name: 'Consultative Approach'

# Consultative Approach: Behavioral Response Definition

## Core Definition

Effective Consultative Approach responses demonstrate genuine curiosity about student needs through thoughtful open-ended questions, persistent probing beyond surface-level answers, and confirming understanding through paraphrasing. These responses enable consultants to gather the information necessary to tailor their approach and ultimately sell the course based on the student's specific motivations and goals.

The effectiveness of consultative approach responses directly impacts enrollment and retention outcomes by:
- **Gathering Essential Information**: Understanding student motivations enables tailored value demonstration
- **Building Trust**: Demonstrating genuine interest through questions and paraphrasing creates psychological safety
- **Uncovering Hidden Needs**: Probing reveals concerns and circumstances that inform better recommendations
- **Creating Student Ownership**: When students articulate their own goals, enrollment feels like their decision
- **Improving Expectation Alignment**: Thorough discovery ensures students understand how the course fits their needs
- **Differentiating from Competition**: Student-focused discovery positions institution above transactional competitors
- **Strengthening Closing Effectiveness**: Paraphrasing before closing makes commitment requests feel natural

## ‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL SCOPE GUIDANCE

This framework focuses **ONLY** on these five key consultative approach response types:

1. Career Motivation Discovery Responses
2. Qualification Background Discovery Responses
3. Outcome Goals Discovery Responses
4. Deep Probing Follow-Up Responses
5. Pre-Closing Value Paraphrasing Responses

Administrative questions, eligibility verification, document requests, and standard information provision without discovery elements fall outside this scope.

## üåü GOLD STANDARD ANALYSIS: THE BENCHMARK FOR ALL RESPONSE EVALUATIONS

The following analysis represents the exemplary standard that should be replicated for ALL consultative approach response evaluations, regardless of specific category type:

> "The consultant's open-ended question, 'What specifically about the program caught your attention?' effectively moves beyond the student's surface-level statement that it 'seems interesting.' This question demonstrates genuine curiosity by focusing on specific interest drivers rather than accepting the vague initial response. The use of 'specifically' is particularly effective as it signals to the student that detailed, thoughtful answers are expected and valued. By asking what 'caught your attention' rather than simply 'why are you interested,' the consultant frames the question in a way that invites storytelling and deeper reflection about the moment or factor that sparked their interest. This approach aligns with the Career Motivation Discovery component by seeking to understand the underlying drivers of their inquiry. The question's open-ended structure ('What specifically...') prevents yes/no answers and requires the student to provide substantive information about their motivations. Successfully gathering this information would enable the consultant to tailor the subsequent course presentation to the specific elements that resonate with this student, significantly increasing the likelihood of demonstrating relevant value and securing enrollment."

## 1. Career Motivation Discovery Responses

**Definition**: Responses that use open-ended questions to explore why students want the course, what career goals they have, what the qualification would mean for their future, or what initially drew their interest.

**Purpose**: To gather foundational information about student motivations that enables tailored value demonstration throughout the consultation.

**‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL LEAD SOURCE RULES**

**For Brochure Downloads:**
- ‚ùå **NEVER USE**: "Do you have any questions about the brochure?"
- ‚úÖ **ALWAYS USE**: Immediate "why" questions
- Example: "What drew your interest to this course?"

**For Web Applications:**
- ‚úÖ **CORRECT SEQUENCE**: Acknowledge application ‚Üí Pivot to "why" before next steps
- ‚ùå **INCORRECT**: Jumping immediately to application details
- Example: "I can see you've submitted an application. Before we discuss next steps, I'd love to understand what motivated you to apply for this course?"

**Key Implementation Principles**:
- Use open-ended "what" or "why" questions, not closed yes/no questions
- Frame questions to invite storytelling and reflection
- Avoid defensive or sales-focused language
- Lead with curiosity, not qualification assessment
- Ask about future aspirations, not just current dissatisfaction
- Connect questions naturally to student's statements

### Exemplary Implementation (Score 5)

#### Example 1: Perfect Web Application Opening

**Call ID**: 625847392015
**Consultant**: Sarah Chen
**Lead Source**: Web Application

**Consultant Response**:
"Thanks for taking my call. I can see you submitted an application for the Master of Business Administration last week - that's great. Before we go through the next steps with your application, I'd really love to understand what motivated you to apply for this particular program. What are you hoping an MBA will help you accomplish in your career?"

**Analysis**:
The consultant's response demonstrates exceptional Career Motivation Discovery by perfectly following the web application lead source protocol: acknowledging the application submission first ("I can see you submitted an application... that's great") before immediately pivoting to discovery with "Before we go through the next steps." This sequence respects the student's demonstrated intent while prioritizing understanding over processing. The question "What are you hoping an MBA will help you accomplish in your career?" is exemplary because it combines three powerful elements: (1) focuses on forward-looking goals ("hoping to accomplish") rather than past dissatisfaction, (2) connects directly to the specific program mentioned (MBA), and (3) frames the question around career impact, inviting the student to articulate concrete professional outcomes. The phrase "motivated you to apply" acknowledges the student's action while seeking the underlying drivers. This approach aligns perfectly with the meeting directive that for web applications, consultants must "acknowledge that they've put their application through and we've received it, and then... just want to understand your motivations are for the course and why you are applying." Successfully gathering this foundational information enables the consultant to reference the student's specific career goals when presenting MBA content, directly connecting program features to articulated aspirations.

**Why This Is Exemplary (Score 5)**:
Perfect adherence to web application lead source protocol, combining acknowledgment with immediate pivot to discovery. The question effectively invites detailed response about career goals while maintaining natural conversation flow. Demonstrates mastery of lead-source-appropriate opening strategy.

#### Example 2: Effective Brochure Download Opening

**Call ID**: 624918472653
**Consultant**: Michael Torres

**Consultant Response**:
"Thanks for downloading the brochure for our Bachelor of Psychology program. What drew your interest to studying psychology at this point in your life?"

**Analysis**:
The consultant's response demonstrates strong Career Motivation Discovery by avoiding the explicitly forbidden brochure question ("Do you have any questions about the brochure?") and instead immediately moving to discovery with "What drew your interest to studying psychology at this point in your life?" This question is highly effective because it combines broad program interest ("studying psychology") with personal context ("at this point in your life"), inviting the student to share both their subject interest and their life circumstances or timing motivations. The phrase "drew your interest" is softer and more curiosity-driven than "why are you interested," making it feel less interrogative and more conversational. The question's structure prevents yes/no responses and requires the student to reflect on and articulate their motivations. This approach aligns with the meeting notes' hard rule that for brochure downloads, consultants must never ask about brochure questions but should "immediately start with 'why' questions." By gathering this information early, the consultant can tailor the entire consultation to the student's specific drivers, whether they're career change, skill development, personal interest, or other motivations.

## 2. Qualification Background Discovery Responses

**Definition**: Responses that use open-ended questions to explore student's educational background, work experience, current qualifications, or related field experience to understand their starting position.

**Purpose**: To understand student's readiness and context for the program, enabling appropriate recommendations and connection of previous experience to course content.

**Key Implementation Principles**:
- Frame questions as "tell me about" or "walk me through" to invite narrative
- Focus on understanding relevance and depth, not just checking boxes
- Connect background questions to course applicability
- Avoid interrogative tone; maintain conversational curiosity
- Ask about experiences rather than just credentials
- Invite student to explain significance of their background

### Exemplary Implementation (Score 5)

#### Example 1: Work Experience Discovery

**Call ID**: 625039485726
**Consultant**: James Patterson

**Consultant Response**:
"You mentioned you've been working in the marketing field. Could you tell me a bit more about your current role and the types of projects you're typically involved with? I'm curious how that experience connects to what you're hoping to get out of this MBA."

**Analysis**:
The consultant's response demonstrates exceptional Qualification Background Discovery by using the invitation structure "Could you tell me a bit more about" which signals genuine interest and creates psychological space for detailed response. The question has two effective components: first asking about "current role and the types of projects" (seeking specific, concrete examples of their work), and then explicitly connecting this to their MBA goals with "I'm curious how that experience connects to what you're hoping to get out of this MBA." This connection is critical because it frames the background discovery not as box-checking but as understanding how their experience relates to their goals, making the question feel purposeful rather than procedural. The phrase "I'm curious" demonstrates authentic interest and humility rather than interrogative authority. By asking about "types of projects" rather than just "role," the consultant invites the student to share specific examples that reveal the depth and nature of their experience, enabling much more tailored discussion of how MBA content would build on or redirect this foundation. This approach gathers both factual background information and the student's own perception of how their experience relates to their study goals.

**Why This Is Exemplary (Score 5)**:
Combines specific background inquiry with explicit connection to student's goals. "Tell me about" structure invites narrative response. "I'm curious" demonstrates genuine interest. Question reveals both experience details and student's own understanding of relevance.

### Qualification Background Discovery Response Scoring Rubric

**5 - Exceptional Implementation**
- Uses "tell me about" or "walk me through" invitation structures
- Explicitly connects background inquiry to student's goals or course relevance
- Asks about experiences and significance, not just credentials
- Demonstrates genuine curiosity through language choices
- Frames questions to invite storytelling and concrete examples
- Gathers information that enables tailored recommendations and connection-making

**4 - Strong Implementation**
- Uses open-ended questions that invite detailed background sharing
- Makes some connection to course relevance or goals
- Shows interest in understanding depth of experience
- May lack explicit connection-making but still gathers useful information
- Invites narrative responses with minor room for improvement

**3 - Competent Implementation**
- Asks open-ended background questions with limited depth
- Shows basic interest in student's experience
- May not explicitly connect background to goals or course
- Gathers factual information but misses opportunity for deeper understanding
- Questions are functional but not particularly engaging

**2 - Basic Implementation**
- Asks background questions that could be answered very briefly
- Shows little interest in understanding significance or depth
- Makes no connection to course relevance or student's goals
- Feels more like credential verification than discovery
- Gathers minimal useful information beyond basics

**1 - Minimal Implementation**
- Uses closed yes/no questions about credentials
- Shows no interest in understanding experience significance
- Treats background as checklist rather than discovery
- Questions feel interrogative rather than curious
- Gathers no actionable information for tailoring approach

## 3. Outcome Goals Discovery Responses

**Definition**: Responses that use open-ended questions to explore what students hope to achieve, what skills they want to gain, how they plan to use the qualification, or what specific outcomes they're targeting.

**Purpose**: To understand student's success criteria and desired outcomes, enabling demonstration of how course content delivers specific results aligned with their goals.

**Key Implementation Principles**:
- Ask about future application of skills, not just acquisition
- Frame questions around concrete outcomes rather than general benefits
- Connect outcomes to career, lifestyle, or personal development contexts
- Invite specificity about what success looks like
- Avoid leading questions that telegraph expected answers
- Ask how they'll measure or recognize success from the qualification

### Exemplary Implementation (Score 5)

#### Example 1: Career Outcome Discovery

**Call ID**: 624857293645
**Consultant**: Elena Rodriguez

**Consultant Response**:
"You've mentioned wanting to move into a management role. What would success look like for you in that transition? I mean, when you think about yourself in that management position, what are the specific capabilities or credentials you'd need to have to feel confident and effective in that role?"

**Analysis**:
The consultant's response demonstrates exceptional Outcome Goals Discovery by using the powerful "what would success look like" framework, which invites the student to articulate concrete success criteria rather than vague aspirations. The follow-up clarification "when you think about yourself in that management position" asks the student to visualize their future self, making the outcome tangible and personal. The question then becomes more specific: "what are the specific capabilities or credentials you'd need to have to feel confident and effective" - this combination of "capabilities" (skills) and "credentials" (qualifications) along with emotional elements ("feel confident and effective") creates a comprehensive picture of what the student needs. The phrase "to feel confident and effective" is particularly powerful because it acknowledges that success includes both competence and self-assurance, giving the student permission to discuss both technical and psychological needs. This multi-dimensional question enables the consultant to later demonstrate how the MBA delivers not just knowledge but also the credibility and confidence the student identified as essential. The question's structure prevents surface-level responses by requesting "specific" elements and asking the student to project themselves into the future role.

**Why This Is Exemplary (Score 5)**:
Uses powerful "success looks like" framework. Asks student to visualize future self in the role. Combines capabilities, credentials, and emotional dimensions of success. Enables consultant to demonstrate comprehensive value aligned with student's articulated success criteria.

## 4. Deep Probing Follow-Up Responses

**Definition**: Responses that probe deeper when students provide surface-level, brief, or vague initial answers, demonstrating curiosity by not accepting incomplete information and asking follow-up questions to uncover underlying motivations.

**Purpose**: To demonstrate genuine interest by persistently exploring student's situation and motivations, transforming vague responses into actionable information that enables tailored approach.

## ‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL DISTINCTION: First Question vs. Follow-Up Probing

**This category ONLY applies to follow-up questions after vague initial responses:**

**‚ùå NOT Deep Probing**: First question asked = Different discovery category
**‚úÖ IS Deep Probing**: Follow-up when initial answer was vague/surface-level

**Example of What's NOT Deep Probing:**
- Consultant asks: "What drew your interest to this course?" (First question = Career Motivation Discovery)
- Student responds: "I'm interested in psychology"
- This initial question is NOT deep probing - it's Career Motivation Discovery

**Example of What IS Deep Probing:**
- Consultant asks: "What drew your interest to this course?"
- Student responds: "I don't know, it just seems interesting"
- Consultant follows up: "What specifically about psychology caught your attention?" (This follow-up IS deep probing)

**Key Implementation Principles**:
- Acknowledge the initial response before probing deeper
- Use gentle, curious language to invite more detail
- Try multiple angles if first follow-up doesn't yield depth
- Avoid making student feel interrogated or defensive
- Frame follow-ups as helping you understand, not testing them
- Demonstrate that detailed answers are valued and useful

### Exemplary Implementation (Score 5)

#### Example 1: Probing Beyond "It Seems Interesting"

**Initial Question**: "What drew your interest to this course?" (segment 34)
**Student Response**: "I don't know, it just seems interesting" (segment 35)

**Call ID**: 625748293014
**Consultant**: Marcus Williams

**Consultant Response**:
"That's totally understandable. Sometimes it's hard to put into words exactly what catches our attention. Let me ask it a different way - what was the moment or situation that made you think 'I should look into psychology programs'? Was there something happening in your work or life that sparked that interest?"

**Analysis**:
The consultant's response demonstrates exceptional Deep Probing Follow-Up by first validating the student's vague initial response with "That's totally understandable. Sometimes it's hard to put into words exactly what catches our attention." This acknowledgment is crucial because it prevents the student from feeling defensive about their incomplete answer while simultaneously signaling that more detail is expected. The consultant then explicitly reframes with "Let me ask it a different way," which transparently acknowledges the first attempt wasn't successful and invites the student to approach the question from a new angle. The follow-up question "what was the moment or situation that made you think 'I should look into psychology programs'?" is particularly effective because it asks for a specific trigger event rather than abstract motivations, making it much easier for the student to identify and articulate. The addition of "Was there something happening in your work or life that sparked that interest?" provides concrete categories (work/life) that further scaffold the student's thinking. This multi-part approach demonstrates persistence without pressure, showing genuine curiosity about understanding the student's story. The consultant transforms a completely vague response ("it just seems interesting") into multiple specific pathways for the student to access and share their underlying motivations.

**Why This Is Exemplary (Score 5)**:
Validates vague initial response without accepting it. Explicitly reframes the question transparently. Asks for specific trigger moment rather than abstract motivations. Provides concrete categories to scaffold thinking. Demonstrates persistent curiosity without creating defensiveness.

## 5. Pre-Closing Value Paraphrasing Responses

**Definition**: Responses that summarize and paraphrase the student's previously stated motivations back to them immediately before requesting enrollment commitment or discussing next steps, confirming understanding and building trust at the critical closing moment.

**Purpose**: To demonstrate that the consultant has genuinely heard and understood the student's specific situation and goals, transforming the closing from a generic transaction to a logical next step aligned with articulated aspirations. This is a **HARD RULE** from the meeting notes - consultants MUST paraphrase before closing as a "trust builder."

**‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL TIMING REQUIREMENTS**

**When This Must Occur:**
- AFTER information provision phase
- BEFORE enrollment closing requests
- Typically in final third of call (segment 100+)
- Immediately preceding commitment or next-step discussions

**When This Cannot Occur:**
- First 5-7 minutes of call (too early)
- Before discovery has been conducted
- During information provision phase
- After enrollment closing has already begun

**Key Implementation Principles**:
- Reference specific motivations student stated earlier in call
- Use student's own language and phrases when possible
- Connect multiple motivation threads into coherent summary
- Frame as confirmation-seeking ("Is that right?" or "Did I understand correctly?")
- Include both "push" (current situation) and "pull" (desired future) factors if stated
- Keep summary concise but comprehensive - 2-4 sentences typically
- Transition naturally from paraphrasing to enrollment discussion

**Critical Meeting Note Reference**:
*"The team discussed the importance of paraphrasing the student's responses to build trust and ensure understanding before closing the sale... we're probably gonna start from, like, a 5% base for the paraphrasing... it's definitely an area that we think we can improve on... If you don't use that information [to close], there's no point getting it."*

### Exemplary Implementation (Score 5)

#### Example 1: Comprehensive Motivation Summary Before Closing

**Context**: Call segment 145 (late stage), consultant approaching enrollment discussion
**Earlier Student Statements**:
- Segment 23: "I want to move into a management role within the next couple of years"
- Segment 45: "I'm currently in a senior coordinator position but feel like I'm missing the strategic skills"
- Segment 67: "Getting this MBA would give me the credibility and knowledge I need"

You've identified that the missing piece right now is those strategic thinking skills that managers need, and you're looking for the MBA to give you both the practical knowledge and the professional credibility that'll help you make that transition. Is that capturing what we've discussed?"

**Analysis**:
The consultant's response demonstrates exceptional Pre-Closing Value Paraphrasing by adhering to the hard rule that paraphrasing must occur "before closing to reiterate the value of the course" as stated in the meeting notes. The opening phrase "Before we talk about getting you enrolled" explicitly signals the transition to closing while creating space for the paraphrasing, preventing the closing from feeling abrupt. The consultant then comprehensively synthesizes three separate student statements from throughout the call (segments 23, 45, 67) into a coherent narrative that demonstrates genuine listening: current position ("senior coordinator role"), timeline ("next couple of years"), gap identification ("strategic thinking skills"), and desired outcomes ("practical knowledge and professional credibility"). The use of the student's own language ("strategic skills," "credibility") makes the paraphrasing feel authentic rather than scripted. The phrase "I just want to make sure I've really understood" positions the paraphrasing as the consultant's responsibility to comprehend rather than the student's obligation to confirm, creating psychological safety. The closing question "Is that capturing what we've discussed?" invites confirmation and creates a natural pause for the student to correct or affirm before enrollment discussion begins. This approach transforms the enrollment request from external pressure into a logical next step aligned with the student's articulated goals.

**Why This Is Exemplary (Score 5)**:
Perfect adherence to hard rule of paraphrasing before closing. Explicitly signals transition with "Before we talk about getting you enrolled." Synthesizes multiple motivations from throughout call into coherent narrative. Uses student's own language. Frames as consultant's responsibility to understand. Creates confirmation moment before closing. Transforms enrollment request into logical next step aligned with stated goals.

- Timing may be off (too early, during closing, or absent entirely)
- Provides minimal summary of motivations
- Shows limited understanding of student's specific situation
- Uses primarily consultant's language rather than student's
- Little or no confirmation-seeking
- Weak connection to enrollment discussion
- May feel perfunctory or scripted

**1 - Minimal Implementation**
- Absent entirely or occurs at wrong time
- Makes no reference to student's stated motivations
- Shows no evidence of listening or understanding
- Jumps to closing without paraphrasing (violates hard rule)
- Creates no foundation for enrollment discussion
- May make student feel unheard or pressured

- Did Career Motivation Discovery early in call enable tailored information provision?

- Did Pre-Closing Value Paraphrasing synthesize all earlier discovery into coherent summary?

This means effective consultative approach must be evaluated both on information gathering AND on subsequent utilization of that information in closing.
